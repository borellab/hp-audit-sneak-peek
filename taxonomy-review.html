The skeleton is right and the bet is clear: shop by scent, not just by product type. Seven of eight doors sell, story is contained, and a scent axis exists at all, which almost nobody in our corpus offers. The sharpest finding is that the bet is not carried far enough: BBW's own prior testing unanimously asked for scent family at the top level, and the draft buries it one level down inside Fragrance. The other risks are concentrated, not spread: Offers is one thin node on the most promo led brand we audit, hand cream has no home, fragrance formats sit as categories where Baymard's highest weighted rule wants filters, the mist vs spray vocabulary crosses L1 boundaries in exactly the way the previous experiment showed costs time and confidence rather than raw success, and there is no bestsellers path while 30 of 73 competitors surface one. The stakes are not abstract: the flyout nav is BBW's top converting element, ahead of search and every asset.

What is strong

▸
Wins first. These are the parts to protect through the test rounds.
Scent gets an axis of its own

By fragrance family plus Learn about scent turns the one thing every BBW product shares into a browsable path. Ten of 46 audited competitors browse by scent family and the standard is five to seven families on a floral, fresh, fruity, gourmand, woody spine. The draft's five families map onto that spine one to one, with warmer names that Yankee Candle proves already read at mass tier.

Shopping first, story contained

Seven of eight L1s are shop doors and the storytelling lives in one Discover door, 12.5% of the nav. That is exactly the corpus average story share and the shape of the healthiest example we audited, Aesop's Library.

Newness has a real home

New in with per category children plus Drops matches how the assortment actually moves. The merch slots for seasonal collections are placeholders by design rather than hard coded labels.

Franchise navigation

Icon collections in the nav treats JCB, ITN, ATW and Gingham as durable franchises, the Rituals Collections playbook, and Yankee Candle runs its eight icon scents through every product L1. It also gives collection landing pages a stable home for SEO.

Gifts would lead the corpus

Recipient, occasion, price, type and gift bags: no competitor runs all of these, and the best today is Molton Brown with recipient plus two price bands plus a gift builder. The current BBW Gifts door already routes by recipient, one price tier and one occasion (verified live: Her, Him, Sets, $20 & Under, Wedding & Bridal Shower, Cards, Boxes & Bags), so the draft extends a working pattern rather than inventing one. Teacher and Medic have no precedent anywhere, unusually specific and very BBW. Recipient based gift paths also have direct Baymard research support, and 90% of sites fail to explain their gifting options in full, an easy place to stand out.

Clear upgrades on the current site

Categories promoted from under Shop to the top level, promo pricing kept out of menu labels (a pattern the audit flags on the live site), Quality replaced by a real Discover tree, and the Fragrance Finder linked from navigation instead of sitting off page.

Risks and open questions

▸
Ranked by priority. This is the standard critique pass every draft earns before testing: each row names the evidence, the research it stands on, and the test that settles it.
PriorityFindingHow to resolve
highScent family sits one level too deep
Prior BBW user testing unanimously required Shop by scent family as top level navigation, and cross listed scent paths hit 97.5% task success while cutting browse to cart time by about two minutes. Zoe shops the whole store by scent profile, yet the draft's only scent entry is an L2 inside Fragrance, invisible to the candle or body shopper.
BBW user testing: scent family unanimous, polyhierarchy 97.5% success · BDO HPT2 readout: Zoe navigates by scent profile · Baymard #274
Tree test the draft against a variant with scent family elevated (a top level entry, or a scent row inside every product L1). Task: find a fresh scented body lotion, then the matching candle.
highOffers is one thin node on a promo led brand, inside an L1 merge nobody else runs
Zero of 73 corpus sites combine newness and deals in one L1, eleven carry both as separate doors. The current site gives Offers its own L1 and the audit shows BBW leads the corpus on promo pressure. All five Jens in the BDO study named a specific promotion unprompted and built baskets around deal eligibility, and the readout's standing rule is that any change reducing deal visibility must pass a Jen gate before it ships. In the draft, that shopper's world is one L2 among six, sharing a door with drops, icons and two campaign slots, six intents in one menu. We put this exact question to Baymard's research assistant: its answer was not to merge them into one peer level category, they are two browse motivations, and if the label stays merged the entry should read as promotional and secondary rather than as a peer of Fragrance or Body.
Baymard #251 · Baymard #1008 · Baymard GC025: deals are the top highlight type at 71% · BDO HPT2: the Jen gate on deal visibility · Baymard research assistant, 14 July
Tree test task: find today's deals. Test the merged door against a split New | Offers variant, then a Jen gate session before the A/B build. Wherever Offers lands, the full research says the sale filter must also exist as a standalone, always visible filter on product lists, never nested inside Price, or shoppers assume no deals exist.
highHand cream has no home
Hands holds only Soap and Sanitizer. Body moisturizer lists cream, butter and lotion with no hand node. BBW sells hand cream at volume and the shopper who wants it has no correct branch, both plausible branches are wrong.
Baymard #263: unambiguous category scope · Baymard #277: cross list where users expect it
Add hand cream (and hand care) under Hands, or rename the L1 scope. Then tree test: buy a hand cream.
highFragrance formats sit as categories where the rules want filters
Eau de parfum, Perfume spray, Mist, Cologne and Travel size are shared attribute subtypes of one fragrance list. Baymard 253, the highest weighted guideline in our extracts, says these resolve as preapplied filters. As separate categories, a shopper cannot compare a mist against an eau de parfum without backtracking.
Baymard #253: subtypes as filters, the central taxonomy rule · Baymard #409: combined lists need strong filters · Baymard #415 · Baymard #417
Implementation rule, not a label change: every format node lands on one fragrance list with a filter applied. Audit the PLPs, then tree test: find a body mist in a sweet scent.
highMist, body spray, perfume spray and scented spray are four labels across two L1s
Fragrance holds Mist and Perfume spray, Body holds Body spray and Scented spray inside Spray & deodorant, Men holds its own Body spray. The distinction is internal product architecture, not shopper language. This is exactly where the previous experiment's pattern (same success, double the time, low confidence) will reproduce.
Baymard #263: no sibling overlap · Baymard #282: shopper language over internal terms · The previous nav experiment: equal success, double time, lower confidence
Tree test tasks phrased in shopper words: a light everyday body fragrance, a fine fragrance spray. Measure directness and confidence, not just success.
mediumGifts (all) duplicates its own L1 and its children repeat a sibling
An L2 named Gifts inside GIFTS, holding For her and For him, while By person holds Her and Him. Two names for the same two audiences invites split analytics, split SEO and shopper doubt. The person and occasion lists themselves were never directly studied, a flagged research gap.
Baymard #263 · Baymard #410: recipient filters were used by 80% of gift seeking participants and beat price filtering, the category is only the signpost · Card sort method: 20 to 30 participants (Tullis and Wood)
Collapse to one recipient facet, then card sort the gift facets (recipient vs occasion vs price vs type) with 20 to 30 gift shoppers.
mediumUnnamed campaign slots inside the tree
Three placeholder slots (two in New & offers, one in Body) hold rotating campaign content. The HPT1 prototype lost 1.33 ease points to exactly this mixed presentation pattern, and rotating campaign names in prime nav positions read as advertising. Keeping them fluid is the plan, keeping them unnamed is the problem.
Baymard #263: category names must be assessable · BDO HPT1: mixed presentation cost the prototype 1.33 ease points
Define the naming rules and a governance gate now, then first click test with realistic campaign names in the slots.
mediumIcon collections live twice under two names
New & offers calls the shelf Icon collections, Body calls it Collections, both list the same four franchises. Two doors to one shelf works well, two names for the same shelf does not. Collection codes also carry no scent information for a new shopper.
Baymard #282: one name sitewide · Baymard #277: cross listing with one canonical destination · Baymard research assistant: collection names carry no scent for new shoppers
One name everywhere, one canonical URL per collection. Zoe comprehension probe: what do you expect behind this link.
mediumInconsistent View all
Home & lifestyle has All last in its list, Soap, Sanitizer and Car lead with All, five L1s have nothing. Baymard 1006 wants a View all at every level, placed first.
Baymard #1006: View all at every level, placed first
Deterministic fix: every level gets All, placed first. Then a menu task: see everything in body care, without search.
mediumHousehold cleaning stretches the tree ahead of the assortment
Kitchen (dish wash, counter spray) and Laundry (detergent, boosters, dryer sheets) give nav weight to categories the brand is only entering. Baymard's floor is roughly ten products per node, and thin shelves behind confident labels erode trust in every other label.
Baymard #261: 10 to 15 per level, about ten products per node
Catalog count audit per node before launch, fold thin ones into a single Home care node until the range fills out.
mediumJargon at decision points
Wallflowers, PocketBac, Blend bar, EDL and FF as collection codes. Loyalists know them, the rebrand explicitly courts new customers who do not. The test plans also warn the reverse: renames tax Jen, whose efficiency depends on familiar names.
Baymard #282: descriptive names over site specific terms · BDO test plans, hypothesis H3: renames tax the loyal cohort
Label comprehension check with both cohorts, pair jargon with plain descriptors in the UI (Wallflowers · plug in fragrance).
mediumCandles moves behind a label that does not say candles
On the current site Candles is one of seven category rows a shopper sees the moment the single Shop door opens. In the draft the shopper must first infer that Home & lifestyle holds candles. Seven of fourteen candle and home fragrance competitors, including Yankee, Nest, Capri Blue and Goose Creek, give Candles a top level word, and BBW leads the category. Same depth in both trees, different inference load.
Audit corpus: 7 of 14 candle competitors keep Candles at L1 · Current site, verified live 14 July: Candles is a visible category row inside the single Shop door
First click test: buy a three wick candle, measured from the closed menu. If shoppers hesitate at the L1 row, surface Candles in the door's visible preview.

Gaps and opportunities

▸
Things shoppers will look for that do not have an address yet, each one is an easy add.
Bestsellers
The corpus's biggest omission: 30 of 73 competitors surface a bestsellers entry, around 19 of them at L1 (Jo Malone, Kiehl's, Molton Brown, Neom, The Body Shop). The draft has no bestseller, most loved or top rated path anywhere, and neither does the current site. It is also the easiest first task for a new visitor.
Hand cream / hand care
Nowhere in the tree. The Hands L1 currently means wash and sanitize, not care.
Bundles and sets
A standing sets or bundles entry runs at 14 of 73 sites (Sephora Gifts & Value Sets, Kiehl's Gifts & Sets, Sol de Janeiro and Saje Bundles). In the draft, sets exist only implicitly inside Gifts. For an AOV machine like BBW, no sets path outside gifting is a real gap.
Refills as a theme
Refills exist as L3s under Soap, Wallflowers and Car. L'Occitane puts Refills at L1 and Yankee runs five refill L2s. A single cross category Refill door is an emerging sustainability pattern and BBW has the range to own it.
Aromatherapy
Only present as an example inside a Body placeholder. It is a named BBW range with wellness intent that spans body and home, decide its address. Mood and wellbeing axes run at 13 of 67 competitors (Neom by wellbeing concern, Cowshed by mood), the one discovery axis the draft skips.
Home fragrance by scent family
By fragrance family lives only under Fragrance. A candle shopper cannot browse warm and woodsy candles without leaving Home & lifestyle, yet Yankee and Voluspa run scent families inside their candle menus. Consider the scent facet as a shared layer, not a Fragrance child.
Sale season handling
Offers has no children. Semi annual sale is the biggest trading moment of the year, decide where it lives before it squats on the merch slots.
Subscribe and save
Wallflowers and car refills are natural subscription anchors and five corpus sites run a subscription entry. Not urgent, but the taxonomy should not block it later.

What the evidence says

▸
The draft checked against the Baymard guidance we hold on file and against BBW’s BDO research readouts.
The stakes

The flyout nav is BBW's top converting element: 24.8% engagement, 95.3K orders and 8.0% CVR in the analytics we hold, ahead of search and every asset CTA. This taxonomy rebuilds the site's highest performing surface, so it earns the full test sequence.

Where the draft follows the rules

Eight L1s sit inside Baymard's ceiling for main categories, and product categories at top level with no generic Shop wrapper is the endorsed pattern.
A New entry for a repeat heavy brand and an Offers entry both have direct guideline support, and deals are the most appealing highlight type in Baymard's own survey (71% vs 40% for bestsellers).
Body as a named L1 is evidence backed white space: in the BDO competitor study, body care was nearly invisible on Ulta and Sephora, found through top nav or not at all.
Gifts by occasion and person answers BBW's strongest research finding: gift discovery failed universally across three age cohorts and five methods, and gift shoppers navigate by recipient.
Hands as a shallow dedicated path serves the proven replenishment task (the BDO benchmark task is literally find a hand soap).
Learn about scent in the nav matches the guideline for thematic guides, kept visually secondary to product categories.
Duplicated doors (Cologne in Men, Learn about scent in Discover) are sanctioned cross listing, provided every duplicate lands on one canonical list.

Where the research urges caution

Filter type entries (formats, Under $20, New, Offers) drawn as real categories: the rules want them as preapplied filters on one list, an implementation decision worth making explicit now.
View all placement is inconsistent and five L1s have none, an easy deterministic fix with an outsized findability payoff.
Rotating campaign content in prime nav slots risks reading as advertising and getting skipped.
Mixed presentation (collections, campaigns and products in one container, as New & offers is drafted) is the measured pattern that cost the HPT1 prototype 1.33 ease points against the live site.
Site specific names at decision points (Wallflowers, PocketBac, Blend bar) run against the plain language naming rule, and the new shopper cohort by definition does not know them.
Jen and Zoe split paths: Jen navigates by deal logic, Zoe by scent and ingredient transparency. The draft serves Jen's door but gives Zoe no browsable ingredient expression and buries her scent axis.
Fresh adherence numbers · Baymard research assistant, 14 July

88% of top US e commerce sites run hover based mega menus, and 31% get the hover behavior wrong (flicker, accidental activation). 37% still hide the catalog behind a generic Shop or Products entry, which the draft rightly avoids. 34% of sites have poor filtering, which matters because every format door in this tree resolves to a filtered list. And 90% of sites fail to explain their gifting options in full, the clearest open goal for the new Gifts door.

The bar to clear

The previous experiment matched the control on success but doubled time on task with much lower confidence. So the tree test must capture more than task success: log directness (first click on the correct branch), time per task, and a per task confidence rating. A tree that people get right slowly and doubtfully fails the launch bar even at high success.

Sourced thresholds: a correct first click leads to roughly 70% task success vs 24% when it is wrong, so first click is the metric to watch. Overall tree test success of 60 to 80% is good, above 90% excellent. Rerun the BDO benchmark tasks verbatim so before and after compare cleanly: find a hand soap and add it to your bag (Jen), find a body care product you would actually buy, without search (Zoe), each followed by the 7 point ease scale. Scores to beat: 7.00 ease on the live site (the prototype managed 5.67), PLP findability 6.8.

Cohorts, per the BDO plans: Jen is a rewards member with 3+ purchases in 12 months, 35 to 60. Zoe has no BBW purchase in 12 months and shops beauty elsewhere, 22 to 38. Run both, on smartphone, in separate pools. The jargon risks live with Zoe, the rename tax lives with Jen.

Usability round

The current UI model may not be compatible with the new structure, so the prototype should stress the two places where the tree meets the interface: the mega menu (eight doors, some with 13 L2 groups, some with 2) and the mobile drawer (three levels of drill in). Baymard's research is reassuring on depth: a three level drill in works when each level is tightly scoped, View all sits first at every level, every item opens on a single tap, and the back path plus current scope stay obvious, so build those rules into the prototype. Their intermediary category page research also matters most on mobile (importance 8.75 there vs 5.0 on desktop), so the prototype should include the landing page an L1 tap opens, not just the menu. Reuse five or six tree test tasks verbatim so the two rounds triangulate, then add interface tasks: recover from a wrong branch, use search after a nav miss, reach Offers from the homepage. Measure the same four numbers plus a simple ease rating.

Gate for the A/B build

The A/B is server side, so engineering builds the new taxonomy once and the timeline is fixed: it needs to be live by September, right after the relaunch, so the improved taxonomy and nav can prove themselves ahead of the Christmas trading period. Working back from that date leaves room for exactly one fix round, which is why validating before building matters so much here.

Three things earn the build slot: the tree test clears its thresholds after one fix round, the usability round surfaces no critical interface issues, and the engineering estimate covers the canonical URL work for the multi parented nodes (18 duplicated labels each need one destination so analytics and SEO stay clean).
